BODAQS (Bicycle open data acquisition system) is an open-source, build-it-yourself data collection
and analysis system for mountain bikes, designed to be accessible to anyone with basic soldering
skills and a curiosity about what their suspension is actually doing.

Every professional gravity team has an engineer watching a laptop full of suspension data.
BODAQS sits between the consumer products that give you a single number and the professional rigs
that require a racing team's budget.
</Lede>

## The problem with suspension tuning

For most riders, suspension setup is a vibe-based, iterative guessing game: adjust a click, do a
run, wonder if that made things better or just 'different', repeat to exhaustion.

At the sharp end of gravity racing, teams solve this with custom data acquisition rigs and dedicated
engineers.
A few consumer products have tried to bridge the gap — ShockWiz, now owned by SRAM, is the standout
example, extracting meaningful suspension insight for most riders in under twenty minutes.

ShockWiz, however is a closed system: the algorithm is proprietary, the data never leaves the
device, and if
your bike performance falls outside what it was designed to answer, you're out of options.

For the rider who wants to go deeper, wants to do more with the data, or whose coil setup isn't
supported,the choice has been: trust a black box, commission professional equipment, or go
without.

## The device

BODAQS is a compact, hackable, open-source data logger you build yourself from widely available
components using basic soldering skills and a 3D printer. It records inputs from suspension travel and other sensors, at up to 500Hz,
capturing rapid events like suspension movement during a landing in fine detail.

A small OLED screen and keypad make it usable on the bike, and an optional
handlebar-mounted button lets you tag moments of interest mid-ride — a heavy landing, a sketchy
section — so they're easy to find later.

Logs are saved as standard CSV text tables: your data is yours,
readable by any tool you choose. The full design is open: PCB schematics, firmware, and
3D-printable sensor mounts are all available to build, fork, and improve.

## The analysis

Recording data is an engineering problem. Deciding what it *means* is the harder part — and the part
that actually matters for tuning.

BODAQS includes a Python analysis framework built on widely used open tools. A preprocessing
pipeline cleans and validates your logs into consistent, comparable sessions. Automated event
detection finds the moments that matter: jJump landings, deep compressions, and fast rebounds —

Comparison tools let you visualise sessions, isolate events, and measure how your setup changes
between runs.

The framework is deliberately transparent: you can see every step from raw sensor signal to output
metric. It isn't designed to hand you a suspension report card. It's designed to help you build real
understanding of what your suspension is actually doing when you are riding your bike, on your
trails, the way you ride.

## Who it's for

BODAQS is designed to scale with the user — in both the build, and the analysis.

If you're new to data acquisition, the hardware guide walks you through assembling your first
logger, and you'll have something real to look at after your first ride with as little as two
sensors.

If you're an experienced Python user, the analysis framework is structured for extension:
define custom event schemas, write new metrics, build new notebooks.

Whether your goal is to get within the wide window of "good enough", to chase optimisation gains at
the sharp end, or simply understand why your bike feels the way it does on that particularly gnarly
corner, BODAQS give you the tools to understand what's happening.
